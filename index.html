About Me

My name is Dailyn Despradel, and as of 2024, I am a third-year Ph.D. student in the Mechanical Engineering Department at Carnegie Mellon University (CMU). I work alongside Dr. Douglas Weber in the Neuromechatronics Lab. My research focuses on understanding the physiological aspects of digitized interactions, particularly within virtual reality (VR) and extended reality (XR) environments.

As an Afro-Latina, I am deeply committed to ensuring representation within VR and XR environments. I strive to develop inclusive and diverse experiences that resonate with a wide range of users. In my free time, I animate and teach middle and high school students how to animate. I have organized events to educate the CMU community about Black experiences through VR and have participated in mentorship initiatives to foster a supportive network among CMU graduate students.

How Do you See the world VR Experience

This project, undertaken by Dailyn Despradel in collaboration with Muzhe Wu and Zixin Yang, aimed to enhance understanding of visual impairments through immersive simulation. We designed an interactive learning experience to educate those with normal vision about the challenges faced by individuals with cataracts, glaucoma, and color blindness. Participants navigated through simulated environments—living room, kitchen, and basement—each designed to replicate the visual obstacles associated with these conditions. Tasks in these environments were carefully crafted based on research to offer a genuine experience of the difficulties encountered in daily life due to these impairments.

In the living room simulation, users experienced cataracts through various everyday tasks, including reading and using household devices. The kitchen environment simulated color blindness, challenging users with tasks like identifying ripe fruits and cooking while relying on color perception. The basement scenario replicated glaucoma's restricted vision through tasks requiring object counting and comparison. After these immersive experiences, participants engaged in a debriefing session, gaining deeper insights into the impairments, their causes, treatments, and impacts. Postprocessing techniques, including depth of field adjustments and color grading, were employed to accurately simulate the visual conditions, enhancing the educational value and fostering empathy and inclusivity among participants. Inclusive/Diverse Hand Gif Library

I (alongside Matthew Weber) have developed an inclusive and diverse hand gesture animation library designed for researchers conducting hand gesture recognition experiments. This library empowers participants by allowing them to choose how they are represented during experimental sessions, promoting personalization and giving them personalization a voice in the process.

The animations were created using three key tools: MakeHuman , Blender , and Krita . MakeHuman enabled the selection of models with the appropriate gender, age, and ethnicity. Blender was used for precise rigging and animation, focusing on wrist and finger movements. Finally, Krita was employed to convert these animations into Graphics Interchange Format (GIFs) for seamless integration into graphical user interfaces (GUIs).

The library includes the following components: It contains meshes representing Caucasian, African, Asian, default, and toon models, with variations for middle-aged and elderly individuals. These models are available for both male and female, including left and right hand variations. The library features animations for finger flexion and extension, wrist flexion and extension, radial and ulnar deviation, as well as wrist supination and pronation. The library, which includes Blender files and GIFs for easy integration into GUIs, can be found in the following GitHub repository.

Meta and CMU Spinal Cord Injury Collaboration

As part of a larger commitment to developing equitable technology, Carnegie Mellon University and Meta have announced a collaborative project to make computer-based tasks accessible to more people. This project focuses on using wearable sensing technology to enable people with different motor abilities to perform everyday tasks and enjoy gaming in digital and mixed reality environments.

Meta is developing an electromyography wristband for consumers that uses sensors placed on the skin to measure the electrical signals the user generates through their muscles, which are translated into input signals for various devices. While Meta has already demonstrated that this technology could replace keyboards and joysticks, the team continues to invest and support different projects to confirm that this technology can be used by a wide range of people.

Neuromechanics of Object Manipulation in VR and The Physical Environment

NEUROMECHANICS OF HAPTIC FREE VIRTUAL INTERACTIONS

Virtual reality (VR) technologies are continuously being adopted as training systems across many domains. Each domain often requires users to interact with virtual objects within the simulated environment. Recent studies have suggested that understanding user-object interaction fidelity can provide essential information about the feasibility of VR as a tool to enhance user performance. Since an implicit goal of all VR systems is to provide users with an experience that is perceived as realistic to improve performance, there is a clear need for metrics that can quantify essential attributes of the user’s interaction with the virtual environment (VE).

To address this need, this work proposes an objective, user-centric metric approach that considers the neuromechanics underlying the user’s behavior that would allow for a quantitative analysis between object manipulations conducted in the real world and a haptic-free VE. It is hypothesized that the movement pattern of object manipulation in a haptic-free VE and the real world are largely consistent. In this pilot study, the examination of specific features of hand kinematics and electromyography during the pre-and-post-contact phases of a pick-and-place task revealed significant differences between the real world and the haptic free-VE. In the future, this paradigm will be applied as a framework to evaluate haptic displays and analyze the technology’s effects on the neuromechanics of user-object interaction toward making VR more realistic.

NEUROMECHANICS OF HAPTIC DRIVEN VIRTUAL INTERACTIONS

This study explores the neuromechanics of user-object interaction within virtual environments (VEs), emphasizing how virtual reality (VR) technology replicates real-world (RW) sensory experiences. Understanding how sensory cues affect behavior and neuromechanics is crucial for optimizing VR applications. By focusing on the interaction between physical and neurophysiological processes during object manipulation, this research aims to deepen our understanding of the neuromechanical foundations of user experiences in VEs.

The study specifically investigates how sensory cues—visual, auditory, and haptic—affect the neuromechanics of VR users and their alignment with RW experiences. The hypothesis posits that incorporating these cues during virtual object interactions would improve finger stability, thereby enhancing motor control during grasping tasks. To test this, we analyzed the effects of sensory cues on muscle activation and hand kinematics during both pre-contact and post-contact phases of object manipulation. Key metrics included maximum grip aperture (MGA), time to MGA (T-MGA), grasp stability, and grasp aperture. Four able-bodied individuals participated in a pick-and-place task involving both physical objects and their virtual counterparts. Hand and finger movements were tracked using a motion-capture system, while muscle activity was recorded using wireless EMG sensors on the forearm.

Comparing user kinematics and EMG data between VEs and RW under different feedback conditions (Visual, Auditory, Vibrotactile, and No Cue) revealed significant neuromechanical differences. In the no-feedback condition during the pre-contact phase, VE showed higher MGA values compared to RW, with longer T-MGA in VE due to visual and motor uncertainties and the absence of physical contact. However, during the post-contact phase, grasp stability and grasp aperture relationships across different feedback types (vibrotactile, visual, and auditory) showed no significant differences from the RW in most conditions and were not significantly different from the no-cue condition. This variability may stem from several factors, including feedback provision, participants' VR expertise, input controllers (such as hand tracking), and the number of trials conducted.

Muscle activity analysis revealed distinct amplitude patterns: higher extensor muscle amplitudes in VE during the no-feedback condition and increased flexor muscle amplitudes during feedback conditions (vibrotactile, auditory, visual). These findings suggest that while sensory cues are vital for guiding motor control in VR, they may not fully replicate the nuanced stability of RW interactions. Challenges such as depth perception uncertainties and the lack of physical feedback in VR environments persist, hindering the achievement of RW-like precision and stability. Further research is needed to address these challenges, potentially through advanced feedback mechanisms or improved VR interfaces.

Ultimately, this study enhances our understanding of the complex dynamics between sensory perception and motor control in VR, highlighting both the potential and limitations of current VR technology in replicating real-world neuromechanics.

CMU Biomechanics Day Outreach Event 2023 - Biomechanics of Hand Rigging and Animation

The 2023 National Biomechanics Day at CMU is an annual, in-person event that introduces biomechanics to high school and middle school students, as well as their teachers. Each year, a team of PhD students organizes interactive sessions featuring various biomechanics topics, allowing participants to engage in hands-on activities and demonstrations.

This year, I was one of the PhD students leading a session, with the support of Prakarsh Yadav. My focus was on teaching students how a deep understanding of biomechanics is crucial for creating realistic animations, and how biomechanics plays a significant role in popular films, especially Pixar movies. Inspired by an exceptional developer's work (link to his/her work), I guided students in animating "Thing" from the show Wednesday. "Thing" is a fictional character, a disembodied hand that, through specific hand mechanics, conveys emotions and actions we typically associate with a person. For example, when "Thing" is angry, it snaps its fingers on the floor, signaling its frustration without the need for words—just movement.

Students were tasked with animating "Thing" as it moves across the screen, similar to the example shown in the GIF below:

By sharing my passion for biomechanics and animation, I aim to inspire these young students to explore the fascinating intersection of science and creativity, encouraging them to pursue similar paths. My involvement in events like National Biomechanics Day reflects my commitment to fostering curiosity and innovation within my community. For more details, visit Biomechanics Day Event Link.

Black History Month CMU MechE DEI VR Event

In early 2024, I had the privilege of leading an immersive session where we introduced university students to the profound meaning of Martin Luther King Jr.'s iconic "I Have a Dream" speech using Oculus Quest 2 VR headsets. The experience, titled MLK: Now is the Time, takes participants on a journey through the key themes of Dr. King’s speech, highlighting the systemic inequities that still persist in our society. Through first-person narratives and interactive elements, MLK: Now is the Time immerses participants in a modern interpretation of a contemporary March on Washington, aiming to inspire a new generation of activists.

Thanks to collaborations with the CMU Mechanical and Biomedical Engineering and Human-Computer Interaction departments, we were able to borrow 20 VR headsets and create an open space where students could engage with the 17-minute interactive experience. Following the session, we facilitated discussions with the students, giving them an opportunity to reflect on the experience and share their insights.

The experience also delved into critical issues such as racial inequality in communities, police brutality, and the systemic disadvantages faced by marginalized groups in voting. For the international students in particular, this session provided a deeper understanding of why Dr. King's speech became such a pivotal moment in history. It underscored the speech's enduring relevance and its powerful message that continues to resonate in the ongoing fight for justice and equality worldwide. This opportunity not only enriched the students' perspectives but also deepened my own commitment to championing diversity, equity, and inclusion. It drove me to take on the role of a DEI Champion, dedicated to fostering understanding and advocating for positive change within our communities.
