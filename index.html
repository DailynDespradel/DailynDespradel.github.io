About Me

My name is Dailyn Despradel, and as of 2024, I am a third-year Ph.D. student in the Mechanical Engineering Department at Carnegie Mellon University (CMU). I work alongside Dr. Douglas Weber in the Neuromechatronics Lab. My research focuses on understanding the physiological aspects of digitized interactions, particularly within virtual reality (VR) and extended reality (XR) environments.

As an Afro-Latina, I am deeply committed to ensuring representation within VR and XR environments. I strive to develop inclusive and diverse experiences that resonate with a wide range of users. In my free time, I animate and teach middle and high school students how to animate. I have organized events to educate the CMU community about Black experiences through VR and have participated in mentorship initiatives to foster a supportive network among CMU graduate students.

How Do you See the world VR Experience

Our project, undertaken by Dailyn Despradel in collaboration with Muzhe Wu and Zixin Yang, aimed to enhance understanding of visual impairments through immersive simulation. We designed an interactive learning experience to educate those with normal vision about the challenges faced by individuals with cataracts, glaucoma, and color blindness. Participants navigated through simulated environments—living room, kitchen, and basement—each designed to replicate the visual obstacles associated with these conditions. Tasks in these environments were carefully crafted based on research to offer a genuine experience of the difficulties encountered in daily life due to these impairments.

In the living room simulation, users experienced cataracts through various everyday tasks, including reading and using household devices. The kitchen environment simulated color blindness, challenging users with tasks like identifying ripe fruits and cooking while relying on color perception. The basement scenario replicated glaucoma's restricted vision through tasks requiring object counting and comparison. After these immersive experiences, participants engaged in a debriefing session, gaining deeper insights into the impairments, their causes, treatments, and impacts. Postprocessing techniques, including depth of field adjustments and color grading, were employed to accurately simulate the visual conditions, enhancing the educational value and fostering empathy and inclusivity among participants. Check out this GitHub repository for the project template and additional details.

Inclusive/Diverse Hand Gif Library

We have developed an inclusive and diverse hand gesture animation library designed for researchers conducting hand gesture recognition experiments. This library empowers participants by allowing them to choose how they are represented during experimental sessions, promoting personalization and giving them personalization a voice in the process.

The animations were created using three key tools: MakeHuman , Blender , and Krita . MakeHuman enabled the selection of models with the appropriate gender, age, and ethnicity. Blender was used for precise rigging and animation, focusing on wrist and finger movements. Finally, Krita was employed to convert these animations into Graphics Interchange Format (GIFs) for seamless integration into graphical user interfaces (GUIs).

The library includes the following components: It contains meshes representing Caucasian, African, Asian, default, and toon models, with variations for middle-aged and elderly individuals. These models are available for both male and female, including left and right hand variations. The library features animations for finger flexion and extension, wrist flexion and extension, radial and ulnar deviation, as well as wrist supination and pronation. The library, which includes Blender files and GIFs for easy integration into GUIs, can be found in the following GitHub repository.
